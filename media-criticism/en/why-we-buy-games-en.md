# Why We Buy Games We Don’t Play

> “I bought the game. Why do I have to play it too?”

Chances are, you’ve had this moment: It’s late at night. You’re in your room, sitting in front of a PC that looks exactly like the dream setup you fantasized about ten years ago. Maybe the latest GPU, liquid cooling humming quietly, RGB lights softly breathing in the dark.

Your Steam library is stacked — hundreds of games. Award winners. Critically acclaimed masterpieces. AAA blockbusters with marketing budgets bigger than Hollywood films.

**You open Steam out of habit.** You scroll. Hover over a cover. Pause. Move away. Hover again.

And then… nothing happens. You don’t click “Play.” You sigh. Close Steam. Turn off the monitor.

A few seconds later, you’re lying in bed, scrolling short videos on your phone — letting cheap, effortless dopamine wash over you. **Easier. Faster. No effort.**

Players have a joking term for this feeling: **“gaming burnout.”**

People who experience it often keep buying games — especially during big sales or hype cycles — but feel strangely resistant to installing or starting them. Their libraries grow faster than their completion lists. Games slowly turn from entertainment into digital collectibles.

There’s even a running joke: 
**“I bought the game. Why do I have to play it too?”**

Faced with a universally praised title, these players feel a subtle psychological friction before double-clicking the icon or picking up the controller. And even if they make it to the main menu, they often quit within minutes — already exhausted by the effort they know the game will demand next.

---

## 1. The Algorithm Trap

Let’s start with a clarification: This isn’t simply a case of “outgrowing games.” What you’re experiencing might be something far more structural — **an algorithm trap.**

Years ago, discovering games was a slow, personal process. You’d browse forums, read niche reviews, or stumble upon titles through friends who shared your taste. Now, that process has been almost entirely replaced by algorithmic feeds. The problem is: **algorithms are exceptionally good at selling you how a game looks — but terrible at telling you how it feels to play.**

Stunning lighting. Cinematic orchestral scores. High-tension CG trailers. These audiovisual elements are universally appealing. They’re easy for algorithms to distribute because they grab attention within seconds. But the core of a game — its interaction — doesn’t translate well into short videos.

* The weight of controls.
* The frustration curve.
* The pacing of its narrative.

Whether failure feels motivating or exhausting — **none of that survives a 15-second clip.**

So what happens is this: You’re making purchasing decisions based on sensory marketing… for an experience that is fundamentally interactive. Under the combined force of streaming platforms and short-form algorithms, certain genres naturally rise to the top. Not necessarily because they’re the most enjoyable to play — **but because they’re the most watchable.**

Highly theatrical “suffering games” — Soulslikes built on spectacle, difficulty, and dramatic reactions. Or live-service titles engineered for constant updates, social buzz, and community visibility. They generate great content. Which means they get pushed harder, you see them more… and also makes them feel like the center of gaming culture.

Over time, this creates an illusion: That these spotlighted experiences represent what gaming as a whole feels like. **They don’t.** They represent what gaming looks like when optimized for spectatorship. 

And this is where the real damage begins. Because eventually, visual attraction pulls you into interaction systems that don’t fit your psychological comfort zone. You load a game because it looked beautiful. Prestigious. Award-winning. Universally praised.

But once you start playing, something feels… **heavy.**

If you seek narrative immersion inside a numbers-driven grind system, you feel drained. If you look for relaxation inside a reaction-punishing challenge game, you feel stressed. The mismatch isn’t dramatic — it’s subtle, but it accumulates. 

**Every session becomes low-grade cognitive labor.** You’re not enjoying yourself. You’re forcing alignment with a system your brain never wanted.

Adults, unlike teenagers, have limited cognitive stamina after work and daily responsibilities. So when gaming starts demanding adaptation instead of offering release, your brain responds defensively. Fatigue appears faster. Resistance builds earlier. Sessions get shorter...

You hover over the “Start Game” button… and hesitate. Not because you don’t want to play. **But because your body remembers the cost.**

And here’s the cruelest part of the algorithm trap: You misdiagnose the problem. You assume:
* “Maybe I just don’t like games anymore.”
* “Maybe I’ve outgrown this hobby.”
* “Maybe masterpieces just aren’t for me now.”

But that’s not what happened. You didn’t lose the ability to enjoy games. **Your enjoyment engine just overheated — running repeatedly in systems that were never built for you.**

---

## 2. The Candy Shell, the Mechanical Core

I know I have fallen into this kind of trap before. I remember watching the trailers for *Elden Ring* — the golden Erdtree glowing against the sky, ruins stretching endlessly into the distance, that orchestral soundtrack swelling like I was about to step into something sacred.

It didn’t feel like buying a game. **It felt like buying a ticket into the “ninth art.”** And for a brief moment, it worked. Booting it up felt cinematic — slow, heavy, important. But the moment I actually stepped into the world, the feeling shifted almost immediately.

The awe faded, and what replaced it was tension. Every encounter felt like a high-stakes negotiation. Every boss fight felt less like adventure, more like trying to decode Miyazaki’s sense of humor — or his cruelty.

And meanwhile, everywhere I looked online, people were posting “no-hit Malenia” clips, or romanticizing suffering as some kind of badge of honor. It created this weird pressure — **like quitting meant admitting I wasn’t a “real player.”** So I stayed longer than I wanted to. Long after the wonder was gone.

And honestly? That kind of forced persistence — playing out of obligation rather than curiosity — might be one of the fastest ways to kill your love for games altogether. When playing starts to feel like self-punishment in service of maintaining a “gamer identity,” burnout isn’t surprising.

**It’s inevitable.**

---

## 3. Be a Real Player

Once you start peeling away the spectacle — the cinematic trailers, the curated clips, the algorithm-fed highlights — something becomes clearer.

What we call “gaming burnout” or even jokingly, “digital impotence,” **often isn’t dysfunction at all. It’s self-protection.**

Your brain quietly refusing to invest energy into experiences that only look rewarding from the outside. You don’t need to fight that feeling. In fact, it might be worth listening to it.

If you can’t bring yourself to launch that critically acclaimed masterpiece, it doesn’t mean your taste is broken. It might just mean your instincts are rejecting an experience that feels like work disguised as play. And in an ecosystem where algorithms constantly try to standardize what counts as a “good game,” one of the most authentic things you can do… is opt out.

Sometimes the more “hardcore” move isn’t finishing *Elden Ring*. **It’s admitting you’d rather spend the evening quietly reorganizing traffic flow in *Cities: Skylines* at your own pace.**

Reclaiming joy starts there — when you take the definition of a “good game” out of the algorithm’s hands, and place it back into your own hands.
